● Known · within team

What the team has decided.

Internal positions reached through reasoning, not user contact. Real artefacts of the team's thinking; not validated against users.

  • Thesis: Kite owns the runtime, not the PR.
  • v1 framework: Next.js marketing sites.
  • Connector: GitHub App.
  • Rejected: "Kite branch" model.
  • Study: Vercel GitHub App pattern.
  • POC target: kiteAI's own Next.js repo.
▲ Assumed · about users

What we believe, on no direct evidence.

Four working hypotheses about who the user is and what they need. They are useful — they let us build. They are not yet research findings.

  • The four user archetypes (create / improve / scale / repair).
  • Their willingness to install a GitHub App.
  • The "snap-speed wow" being the deciding moment.
  • That non-technical users will trust Kite with deploy.
  • The ordering of the proposed funnel.
■ Unknown · need data

What we have no read on at all.

Gaps that materially change the product if they resolve differently than we assume. These are the questions a single afternoon of interviews would start to close.

  • Which archetype is the modal early customer?
  • Does the credit-card-before-GitHub funnel convert?
  • What trust threshold gates the install?
  • How often do real changes span multiple repos?
  • What does churn look like after month one?
Section · 01 of 03

Four working hypotheses about the user.

Each card is a falsifiable claim, not a finding. Confidence levels reflect how strong the evidence currently is — for all four, that is "Low" until interview data comes in. Treat these as the smallest set of bets the v1 build is making on the user.

4 hypotheses
all currently · LOW
H1
Create · greenfield Solo founder archetype

there is a meaningful pool of founders who want a marketing site live in one session, will accept a Kite-owned runtime to get it, and will value GitHub ownership enough that we should ship it on day one.

Implied by the thesis but not surfaced anywhere in the Slack thread. The closest analogue in the wild is the Lovable funnel — which converts on prompt-to-prototype, not prompt-to-production-marketing-site.

Confidence
Low
n = 0 interviews
iCurrent evidence

Analogous: Lovable's adoption curve for prompt-to-prototype. No first-party evidence that the same demand exists for prompt-to-production-marketing-site.

×Would be falsified by

Interviewees telling us they would rather pick Vercel/Framer/Webflow and own infra choices themselves; or that they don't need "live by tonight" — they need "live by next month, done right."

→How to validate

5 problem interviews with pre-PMF founders who launched a marketing site in the last 90 days. Map: what they used, what hurt, where they paid.

!What it changes if false

Greenfield drops out of v1. Scenario B becomes a v2 problem. v1 ships only the import-existing-repo flow.

H2
Improve · existing repo In-house marketer archetype

in-house marketers at companies with a Next.js site and an engineering team will install a GitHub App to ship copy changes themselves, bypassing the engineering ticket queue.

The most strategically important hypothesis — Scenario A is the primary v1 bet. We have no evidence that marketers (rather than engineers) will own the install decision, or that engineering teams will allow it.

Confidence
Low
strategically critical
iCurrent evidence

Anecdotal: the "marketer waits for the next release train" pattern is widely complained about. No data on who actually authorises a GitHub App install in practice.

×Would be falsified by

Marketers saying their VP-Eng or platform team will refuse a third-party GitHub App on a production repo; or that they already have a CMS workflow that's "good enough."

→How to validate

5 interviews each with marketers and their VP-Eng / platform leads at Series A–B companies. The split matters — both sides have veto power.

!What it changes if false

v1 retargets to founder-led companies where the marketer is the engineer. GTM motion changes from PLG-for-marketers to PLG-for-founders.

H3
Scale · ongoing experiments Growth lead archetype

growth leads will adopt Kite as their default experimentation surface if it persists context — brand voice, components, "do-not-touch" rules — across weeks of changes.

This is the retention hypothesis. H1 and H2 are about adoption; H3 is about whether a user comes back next week. It also makes the strongest demand on Kite's memory and state model.

Confidence
Low
retention-critical
iCurrent evidence

None. We have no data on whether growth leads currently exist as a distinct buyer in our ICP, or whether their workflow is monolithic ("just give it to engineering") in practice.

×Would be falsified by

Most companies in our ICP not having a dedicated growth function; or growth running experiments in a different layer (Optimizely, GrowthBook) rather than touching the site code at all.

→How to validate

Defer. H3 is a retention hypothesis — testable only after H1/H2 produce activated users. Validate via cohort behaviour at month 1, not interview.

!What it changes if false

v1 still ships, but the memory/persistence engineering bet is right-sized down. Without H3, Kite is a one-and-done tool, not a SaaS.

H4
Repair · diagnose & fix Non-technical owner archetype

there is a viable wedge in non-technical owners of existing sites who will pay Kite to diagnose problems they can describe in plain English and ship fixes without becoming developers.

The wildest of the four hypotheses — and potentially the highest-leverage acquisition wedge. Also the one most likely to expose the limits of Kite owning a runtime it didn't build.

Confidence
Low
speculative wedge
iCurrent evidence

None first-party. Indirect: the existence of "fix-my-site" agencies suggests demand exists at some price point. Unknown whether that price supports SaaS economics.

×Would be falsified by

Owners saying they don't perceive the problems we'd find ("it works for me"); or that they trust an agency over an AI for anything touching their live site.

→How to validate

Skip interviews. Lightweight landing-page test: "free site audit by AI." Measure email signups and conversion to a paid fix. n = 200 visits target.

!What it changes if false

v1 drops the repair scenario. The remaining product is improve + create, both targeted at people who already know they want to ship changes.

Section · 02 of 03

Decisions already on the record.

These came out of internal reasoning, not user research. They are real positions the team holds — listed here so the hypotheses above can be debated against them, and so we know which decisions need to be reopened if the hypotheses fail.

Position log
Thesis
Kite owns the runtime, not the PR. "Just raise a PR" creates infinite scope; if Kite doesn't control build, preview, and deploy, it can't control the experience, even with correct code.
Accepted
v1 framework
Next.js only. Marketing-shape sites only. Other stacks deferred until the loop runs well on one stack.
Accepted
Connector
GitHub App, used in both scenarios (create new, improve existing). No GitLab, no Bitbucket in v1.
Accepted
Branch model
Branch off main per change, merge into main on approval. "Kite branch" rejected — double publish is bad UX.
"Kite branch" out
Environment parity
DP and production environments must be identical — both Kite-controlled — or the wow moment rents reliability from infra we don't own.
Accepted
Reference pattern
Vercel's GitHub App: install creates DPs, exposes a production URL that may or may not be customer's real prod. Same architectural shape as our problem.

Section · 03 of 03

The smallest research program that turns the assumptions into evidence.

Sequenced so that the cheapest, highest-leverage learning happens first. Each phase has a decision trigger: an explicit "go / pivot / kill" rule, so research outputs change the build rather than decorate it.

4 phases
~6 weeks
Sequenced phases
Phase 01 · week 1–2

Problem interviews

10 calls. Five with in-house marketers at Series A–B companies; five with their VP-Eng or platform lead. Focus on H2 — the primary v1 bet.

OutputGo / pivot on H2
Phase 02 · week 2–3

Dogfood on kiteAI

Run the loop end-to-end on our own Next.js marketing site. Five real changes Subhrashis would have shipped this month. Instrument latency and friction.

OutputLoop time-to-preview benchmark
Phase 03 · week 3–4

Founder & repair signal

5 founder interviews for H1. A landing-page test for H4 ("free AI site audit"). Both are cheap; both can run in parallel with the alpha build.

OutputKeep or cut H1 & H4
Phase 04 · week 5–6

Closed alpha · 5 users

Hand-picked from existing network. Instrumented funnel from install to first deployed change. Cohort-tracked into month 1 to start reading H3 retention.

Unknowns ranked by decision-impact
Modal user
We do not know which of the four archetypes is the most common early customer. This determines GTM and the ordering of the funnel. Cheap to find out in Phase 01; expensive to discover at GA.
High impact
Install authority
In Scenario A, the marketer asks for the install but the engineer grants it. We have no read on the engineer's willingness to allow a third-party GitHub App on the production repo. If the engineer says no, the loop never starts.
High impact
Funnel ordering
Current draft: pre-analysis → report → credit card → GitHub → verification → charge. The card-before-install ordering is plausible but untested. Worth A/B at alpha.
Med impact
Multi-repo reality
v1 says single repo per thread. We do not know how often real marketing-site changes touch a content repo and a code repo. If common, the v1 promise breaks early.
Med impact
Exit ergonomics
A clean exit from Kite when Kite has owned the runtime is undefined. Low impact for alpha — high impact before GA. Worth a one-page spec before the first paying customer.
Low (now)
