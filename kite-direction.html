Research consultant 12:14 PM
Reframe

One reframe before we go in. "Migrate the codebase to our infrastructure" is being used to mean two different things in the thread, and they fail differently.

  1. Migrate-the-code — copy or fork the customer's repo into a Kite-owned Git host. The source of truth moves.
  2. Migrate-the-runtime — leave the source of truth in their GitHub, but run build, preview, and deploy on Kite infra.

The thesis you posted argues for (2). Arpit's Lovable prototype implies (1). Abhishek's GitHub-app suggestion is closer to (2). They've been blurred together. Which one are we deciding on?

Research consultant 12:18 PM
Surface assumption

Four assumptions, and the decision rests on all of them. None has been tested with a real user.

  1. Speed dominates trust. Users want Kite's snap-speed preview badly enough to let a third party drive build and deploy on their production site.
  2. Install authority converts. Whoever wants Kite (often a marketer) can actually get the GitHub App installed — the VP-Eng or platform team approves the permissions.
  3. Runtime parity is achievable. Kite can replicate the customer's existing environment closely enough that "works on the Kite DP" reliably implies "works in production."
  4. Exit is acceptable. Users tolerate the lock-in implied by Kite owning the runtime, because GitHub-as-source-of-truth gives them a credible "I can leave" story.

If any one of these is false, the migrate-the-runtime answer weakens. If two are false, it probably collapses.

Which one would you bet against first?

Research consultant 12:20 PM
Challenge

Number two. Install authority. That's where I'd put the first interview hour.

The thread treats "user installs the GitHub App" as a single act. It isn't. At any company past five engineers, installing a third-party GitHub App on a production marketing repo is an engineering-org decision. The marketer asks; the platform lead grants. We've been imagining a one-step funnel where it's actually a two-step one with a different person at each step.

If the platform lead routinely says no — for security, audit, or "we don't allow third-party deploy keys" reasons — then Scenario A (improve existing repo) never starts, no matter how good the loop is.

Research consultant 12:22 PM
Validate · propose a test

The cheapest way to get a read on it:

  • 5 marketers at Series A–B companies who own a Next.js marketing site. Ask: when you last needed to ship a copy change, what was the bottleneck? Who would you need to ask before installing a GitHub App on the site repo?
  • 5 platform leads / VP-Engs at the same kind of company. Show them the Kite GitHub App permission scope on a screenshot. Ask: would you approve this? What would change your answer?

Two weeks, ten conversations. If 7+ of the platform leads say no on the spot, we have an answer. If they're split, we have a wedge: which kind of company says yes.

Kill criterion
7 or more "would not approve" responses from platform leads → the GitHub-App-as-deploy-agent model needs a redesign before v1 ships. Possibly a "Kite GitHub App, read-only by default; deploy is opt-in per repo" variant.

Subhrashis Das 12:23 PM

What about Scenario B? Greenfield. There's no platform lead because there's no company yet. Does this assumption matter there?

Research consultant 12:24 PM
Reframe · differentiate scenarios

Right — Scenario B doesn't have the install-authority risk. The solo founder is both the marketer and the platform lead. They approve their own GitHub App.

But B has a different load-bearing assumption that A doesn't: the user wants to stay in Kite long-term, not just use it to launch and leave. A Lovable-style user might prompt-to-prototype, hit "Export to GitHub," and never come back. If that's the dominant pattern, we built a runtime to serve a workflow that doesn't need one.

So the two scenarios fail differently. A fails at install. B fails at retention.

S
Subhrashis Das 12:25 PM

The Vercel reference in the thread — does that actually help our case, or are we just citing it because it's familiar?

Research consultant 12:26 PM
Challenge · qualify evidence

The Vercel pattern proves the architecture is possible — a GitHub App that takes over preview deploys and exposes a Vercel-controlled production URL. It does not prove that our target user wants the same thing.

Two different claims:

  • Engineers at companies that adopt Vercel have already accepted the trade. They chose the framework because Vercel deploys come with it. That population is self-selected.
  • Marketers at companies that didn't choose Vercel — and whose engineers might have chosen something else on purpose — are a different audience. Vercel's adoption tells us nothing about them.

Cite Vercel for "this architectural shape exists in production at scale." Don't cite it for "users want this."

S
Subhrashis Das 12:27 PM

Anything in the thread we should be more worried about than we are?

Research consultant 12:28 PM
Surface · unnamed risk

One thing the thread didn't name: the customer's "extras."

A real production marketing site often has things that aren't in the repo — a CDN config, a CMS hook, an Optimizely tag, a Segment snippet, an auth proxy for a gated section. When Kite imports the site into its own runtime, those pieces may or may not come along.

If they don't, the Kite-preview will look right and the Kite-production will look broken. The user will blame Kite, correctly. "Works on the Kite DP, breaks in prod" is the failure mode the thread was trying to avoid in the first place — and we've reintroduced it through a different door.

This isn't a reason to reject the migrate-the-runtime answer. It's a reason to make "what's in the customer's runtime that we don't see" an explicit part of the import checklist.

Subhrashis Das 12:29 PM

So what's the recommendation? Do we migrate the runtime — yes or no.

Research consultant 12:30 PM
Recommend

Yes — migrate the runtime, not the code. Don't reopen the decision; the argument holds.

But ship it conditional on two things being true, which you can find out in two weeks:

  1. Install authority is not a hard no. Test via 10 interviews (5 marketers + 5 platform leads). Kill criterion: 7+ platform-lead "no"s.
  2. The "extras" inventory is real. Before alpha, run an import on three real-world Next.js marketing repos — not ours. See what doesn't come across. If more than half the imports drop something the customer would notice, the import checklist needs to be a product surface, not an internal one.

And one thing to push to v2 deliberately:

  • Greenfield retention. Don't try to find out in week one whether Scenario-B users stay in Kite. You'll only know that at month one. Build for it; validate it after.
